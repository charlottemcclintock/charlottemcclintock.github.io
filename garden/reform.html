how to make change

abolitionist steps vs. reformist reforms


Critical Resistance's charts on abolitionist steps vs. reformist reforms in policing and prisons are such a useful way to understand how we might think about what gets us closer to dismantling the system vs. what entrenches it further.

I've been thinking about public use of data (more on that here) and how it fits into this kind of framework.

I worry that data encourages marginal thinking and constrains solutions. Showing that Black people are overrepresented in jails and prisons by 5 times suggests that there is a correct number of Black people to be kept in cages by the state, and that our goal should be racial parity in the prison population through a series of marginal reforms aimed at the overrepresentation but not the structural violence of the prisons, police, and jails.

Can data be used in service of abolition?

I've been thinking about this in the context of organizing to defund the DC Metropolitan Police Department. A quick analysis of recently released data shows that 75% of all people and 87% of the children stopped by MPD in the first half of 2020 were Black. 80% of white people who were stopped got some sort of ticket, compared to less than half of Black people, which pretty clearly shows the cops are stopping and harassing Black people (and children) without any cause to actually ticket them.

Are these stats something that should be central in the discourse around defunding MPD? Or does putting them there legitimize this abstract, aggregated form of information which depersonalizes the harm and suggests marginal solutions? How can we talk about these numbers in a way that makes it clear that only structural changes will suffice for the injustice that is evident?
