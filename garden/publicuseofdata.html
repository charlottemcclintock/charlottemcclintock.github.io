fearmongering about violent crime

public (mis)use of data


I've been thinking a lot lately about the utility of data in fighting for the abolition of the prison industrial complex. We live in a world where quantitative data is valued over so many other ways of knowing, but quantitative data is so limited in its descriptive capacity and so prone to misuse.

In my recent work for the ACLU, I saw the danger of incomplete description of the data. The Colorado Department of Public Safety released a press release on the 2020 crime data which stated that violent crime increased by 6.5%, emphasizing increases in murder and aggravated assault. Local news outlets immediately ran with the story of big increases in violent crime (The Denver Post article also importantly noted the likely influence of these numbers on criminal legal reform measures in the upcoming legislative session).

The Colorado Department of Public Safety report relied on the FBI Uniform Crime Reporting definition of “violent crime” which includes murder, aggravated assault, non-consensual sex offense, and robbery, but importantly does not include simple assault (though many other federal definitions do). This omission is important because the overwhelming majority of violent crimes are simple assaults, which declined from 2019 to 2020. When simple assaults are included, the data shows that overall violent crime did not increase and in fact decreased by 0.3% from 2019 to 2020. The increase in aggravated assaults is largely balanced out by the decrease in simple assaults (as this figure shows), along with declines in non-consensual sex offenses.

The ACLU is running a jail decarceration bill this session and the crime trends will factor heavily in a discussion on the impact of COVID-related jail decarceration on crime will be a major point of discussion in rallying support (this figure shows there was no association between jail decarceration and crime rate).

Omitting simple assault and only having public discourse about the types of assault which increased is potentially damaging to the probability of passing the decarceration legislation. Local media uncritically ran with the Colorado Department of Public Safety's incomplete and misleading characterization of trends in the data. We need more data capacity in the public interest to interrogate state narratives of data trends (especially in criminal legal data).
