Jenny Odell

How To Do Nothing


I loved this book. The author describes the book near the beginning as more of a walk with a friend than a lecture, and it reads that way. The highest praise I can give a book is the it has somehow changed how I move through the world, and this book pretty immediately changed my attention economy habits and my attention to bioregional place. Odell writes, “To do nothing is to hold yourself still so you can perceive what is there.”

It sounds trivial and obvious, but I’ve realized that life is composed of days - yeah, there are the big milestones, but most of what composes our conscious lives is the in between - the lazy afternoons and busy mornings. Odell describes how the attention economy creates “the difficulty of maintaining any level of silence or interiority” and that resonated so deeply with me - I feel like over the past few years my thoughts have become more scattered and fragmented as I’ve replaced the quiet parts of my day where I might think and reflect with podcasts, Netflix, or TikTok and instead of sitting with the briefly uncomfortable transition to consuming ideas to engaging with my own, I often turn to the easy pleasure of manufactured content. I’m trying to unlearn this pattern and regain my interiority.

She quotes some Italian philosopher on the idea that an alternative to repression of dissent is proliferation of useless chatter and this certainly rings true of the social media algorithms I’m on. Twitter was a significant part of my political radicalization in seeing some ideas that I had uncritically internalized challenged for the first time, in ways that made me read more and learn more and ask questions and change my beliefs. But all of the constructive work of building my politics has happened off the internet - it’s so much easier to make a pithy point in a small number of characters than it is to reckon with the complexity and nuance inherent in healing, building, growing, and maintaining.

I also really liked her ideas of placefulness and bioregional awareness as a way to be deeply and locally present and aware. Ro got me a book on tree identification and I’ve been using the iNaturalist app she mentions in the book to identify spring flowers in DC and it’s been so meditative and joyful - I feel like I’m connecting the curiosity and attention I had as a kid. I understand more now what Robin Wall Kimmerer talked about in Braiding Sweetgrass when she remarked on how alone she would feel without her knowledge of the plants and ecology around her that gave her a sense of place and community - I’m slowly emerging from that ecological loneliness.
