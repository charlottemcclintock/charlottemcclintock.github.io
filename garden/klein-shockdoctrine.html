Naomi Klein

The Shock Doctrine

holy shit this book. it's such a compelling analysis of recent political and economic history and the rise of neoliberalism, following it through Chile, Argentina, Bolivia, Poland, Russia, South Africa, Iraq, Sri Lanka and more. Naomi Klein characterizes neoliberalism as a fundamentalist faith, resistant to learning from the disastrous consequences of the actions of devout believers, where the answer is always more privatization, less protections for labor, the earth, and people. her characterization of a future of disaster apartheid, privatized security in walled suburbs, and a guns to caviar index where instability and profits move together feels eerily similar to the world Octavia Butler builds in Parable of the Sower.

she talks about debt scare tactics and deficit mongering in Canada used to cut social services and the way international financial commitments to the IMF and the WTO keep South Africa from enacting the promises of the ANC's Freedom Charter. It feels like a useful lesson in cutting through the bullshit arguments people have on the face of a debate and down to the core power struggle.

she also ends the book talking about ways to become "shock proof" and pulls on examples of salvage in capitalist ruin in Argentina and Brazil, the need for hyperlocal democracy in daily life (co-ops, organizing), and importance of work led by groups with a deep sense of place who know how to work within the context. "These are movements that do not seek to start from scratch but from scrap, from the rubble that is all around."
