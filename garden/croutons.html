a very short essay on

croutons and late capitalism


It is the absolute silliest things that send me into a spiral about the world in which we live. Most recently croutons. Before that, helium and gyms.

In learning to cook in the pandemic, figuring out what to do with stale bread lead to the discovery of how to make croutons at home. Croutons are wildly simple - slightly stale bread, oil, and spices; they're an ingenious way to turn something that might otherwise be waste into something useful again. And for some reason the fact that for all of my life I have been buying croutons hit me especially hard. It seems somewhat emblematic of my (our?) general disconnection from the roots of the practices, ideas, beings, and objects around me. Buying croutons from a store in which they are manufactured from scratch explicitly to meet the consumer need from something which originally prevented waste seems almost obnoxiously silly.

Helium has a similar effect on my brain. Helium, as it turns out is mined from the earth and is a deeply finite resource - because its atomic radius is so small, it escapes its container and even escapes the atmosphere of the earth. The earth is running out of helium, one of the most abundant elements in the universe. Liquid helium is essential to MRI machines. We have a limited amount of helium for all of the people and being that will ever live on this planet for all time and we're still putting it into baloons.

I've always hated gyms, but there's something specific about the way in which gyms capture the dystopia of the present. People pay money to consume electricity on metal and plastic manufactured machines so they can prevent their bodies from atrophying because the rest of their lives are so sedentary. That feels very dark to me. If there were no other problems, I'd spend some time finding a way to create community pathways for the allocation of physical labor such that a couple strong young people who want to work out are connected with an elder neighbor who needs a piano moved up a few flights of stairs out of a basement or with a single parent who needs groceries from a store a few miles away. There must be a better system than gyms.

I can't quite articulate yet why all of these ideas are related in my brain, except to say that they feel as though they are, and so far that's enough for me. Something about the dream of infinite resources and the disconnection from the constraints of biological and terrestrial reality feels emblematic.
