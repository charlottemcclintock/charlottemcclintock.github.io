Race & Homelessness in Charlottesville

I worked with the Thomas Jefferson Area Coalition for the Homeless (TJACH) in Charlottesville to investigate racial disparities in the provision of local homelessness services. This collaboration came out of a happy coincidence meeting with the exective director of TJACH, where he expressed a desire to use their data to understand racial equity in the provision of homelessness services but noted that they lacked the technical capacity to undertake the research. I was able to connect him to the PIs on a grant through the UVA library which had funding to support community efforts to build data capacity in service of equity in the region. This became the pilot project on that grant and I worked as the principal researcher.

After completing a report on racial disparity across the system of service providers, I also continued the project as a graduate thesis (Assessing Vulnerability in Homeless Individuals & Families: An Application of Factor Analysis & Item Response Theory). The VI-SPDAT (Vulnerability Index Service Prioritization Decision Assistance Tool) is widely used by homelessness service providers to assess vulnerability in homeless individuals and families and determine who to prioritize for housing. The analysis explored data from the VI-SPDAT as administered in Central Virginia to assess whether the tool appropriately assesses vulnerability among unhoused people using classical test theory, item response theory, and factor analysis. I found that current item groupings collapse valuable information and that re-scoring of the assessment is potentially appropriate and suggested different groupings of items for interpretable subtests to inform providers about aspects of client vulnerability.

The report and thesis will be published here when they are made public.
